# Chapter Five: Travelers with Secrets

The day before Bonding Day. Calderis was a pot about to boil.

From the rim of the volcano looking down — festival banners hung from the top all the way to the Rift's edge. Steam from the Forge Hall burst from the middle cliffs, refracting through the Prism Studio's illusion flares into brief rainbows. The Lantern Tower's spheres blazed in unison, their citation networks churning. The Foundry's spiral ramps floated dozens of progress displays, tomorrow's samples working their way through final finishing. The whole city was a machine cranked to its highest gear.

Kaeden reached the outer city at noon.

He jumped off the cart, cloth bag over his shoulder. Two changes of clothes, his mother's pickles, a stone pried from a wall in Gray Bridge. Still warm. He stood at the city gate and looked up for a full minute. Lights embedded in the crater wall like an inverted night sky. He'd seen this in the ceiling cracks, but the cracks had only given him a blurred sketch. The real Calderis had color. Sound. Smell. Sulfur from the forges drifting from somewhere above. Refraction flares from the Prism Studio sliding past his feet — cat-sized, rainbow-colored, possibly sentient. The Lantern Tower's peaks drifting slowly overhead like old men playing chess, each move considered at length.

He touched the scar above his eyebrow. Blue lines were everywhere — in the ancient stone of the outer wall, on the rim of a well, on the work clothes of a forge apprentice passing by. Calderis had far more blue lines than Gray Bridge, and far more noise. He was nearly drowning in perception. He had to concentrate just to find the road in front of him.

He took a bed at a cheap inn near the gate. Then went out. Not to find anything. To let the blue lines tell him where to go. Eyes closed, the signals sharpened. The nearest one, the brightest — aimed toward the Rift. The Abyssal Platform.

Halfway there he saw a girl. Thin, dark hair, bulging pockets. She moved through the crowd at double speed. Her eyes didn't track the banners or the illusions — they tracked the ground, the corners, other people's pockets. Then she looked up. Between her and Kaeden: a chestnut vendor. Their eyes crossed. No pause. But her pockets were glowing. Blue.

Kaeden took two steps in her direction. She glared at him and vanished into the crowd. The blue afterimage stayed. Her pocket-glow — same frequency as the stone in his bag.

Orion was setting up at the exhibition grounds.

His father arranged the samples. Pots. Hoes. Mirrors. Door rings. Useful things, made better than anyone else's. Iron Kiln had no guild brand — it survived on reputation. Orion unpacked crates nearby. His hands were steady but his mind wasn't in his chest. It was in his pocket. The meteorite was hotter this morning than it had ever been. Not burning. A warm hand on his chest. It wanted him to know it was here.

He set down the last piece. Straightened up. Sunlight slanted from the rim, Prism Studio flares drifting across the exhibition grounds. One landed at a girl's feet. Glasses, copper wire repair on the bridge. A stray lock of hair. Three books in her arms. She tucked the top one under her armpit to free a hand for her glasses. Then she saw the copper pot on their display table. She looked at it the way no buyer looked. Buyers looked at the price. She looked at the seams — the join between handle and body, the curve of the spout, the bottom for uneven hammer marks. Then she looked up. Her lenses caught a faint blue reflection. Not from the pot. From his pocket.

"What's in your pocket." Not a question. A statement.

"Ore. Rift-side."

She paused one second. "This afternoon, Probaris is doing a demonstration at the Abyssal Platform. You should go." Turned. Walked away. No introduction. No waiting for an answer. Three books, copper-wire glasses, that stray lock bouncing against her shoulder. She hadn't come for the exhibition. She was hunting. His meteorite wasn't what she was looking for — it was her confirmation. He'd seen that look in her eyes. Not curiosity. Verification.

He put his hand on the meteorite. Pulsing faster than this morning.

Aella packed her things at the Lantern Tower.

Her dorm was in the basement of the Northwest Tower. The sphere had been dead for thirty years. Nobody wanted to live here. The upside: the corridors never moved. No active citation relationships to reorganize. She hid the forbidden book inside her uniform. The bookmark still in chapter one. She'd read every night for two weeks. The structure of the Seal. The seven creators. The name that had been crossed out in a single stroke. The line — "The Seal is not made of stone or magic. The Seal is a lie." Every time she read it she paused. Not because she didn't understand. Because she understood too well. Her red threads, her problem fragments, her spiral — and that sentence. Same thing. Someone was covering something up. Not one person. A system. From the day the Seal was built to now.

She passed Scriba's office. Door open. Empty. His books lay open on the desk — not blown there, opened to specific pages on purpose. One page had a line on it. Not his handwriting. Older. Ink faded but retraced by someone. "Kaelen. If one day I'm gone. Don't seal it. Teach it. — Veralis." Aella stood in the doorway. She'd never known what Scriba looked at every day. Now she knew part of it.

She touched the forbidden book through her uniform. Walked back to her dorm. Tomorrow. Bonding Day. The Abyssal Platform.

Nova counted pieces under her bridge.

Not accounting — she wasn't selling anymore. Looking for rules. Every piece had a different break edge. Not shattered. Pried apart. Someone with a specific tool had dismantled a single complete object into parts. She sorted by break pattern. Whoever did this hadn't been destroying randomly. They'd been methodical. Like a butcher jointing meat.

Then she noticed a piece she'd missed before. The smallest one, at the bottom, no blue. Not dark — dead. She flipped it over. On the back, a mark, tiny, nearly eaten away by corrosion. She pulled out the micro-prism the hooded man had given her and held it over the mark. The prism light made the engraving visible. A letter. V.

She put it in the bundle. The bundle trembled once. Confirmation. Like it was saying — you found the first one.

Outside, the festival banners rustled in the night wind. Kaeden turned over in his inn bed, the stone in his bag radiating heat. Orion touched the meteorite in the exhibition tent — it pulsed at the same rhythm as his father's hammer. Aella laid the forbidden book and a red thread side by side on her desk. Two forms of answer. Nova stuffed the bundle into her inner pocket. Not throwing it away again.

Not yet dawn. But all four of them were waiting for the same thing.
