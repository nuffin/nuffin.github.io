# Chapter One: Three Days

Kaeden discovered the wall was talking to him while he was moving the pickled vegetable jars.

Well. Not exactly talking. Walls don't have mouths. There were three blue lines on the wall. People in Gray Bridge called them water stains. The bridge was old, the riverbed had been dry for who knows how many centuries, and moisture seeped out of the stone, leaving these vein-like marks. Everyone found this explanation satisfactory. Nobody could see the blue lines.

Kaeden could. Always had.

But until now all he'd done was see them. They sat there quietly, like birthmarks on the wall. Today, the bottom of a jar knocked against the stone step. Nothing loud. Just a dull thud, the same sound jars always made hitting the ground. But the wall flickered. All three blue lines at once. Then something entered Kaeden's ear. Not a sound. His eardrums stayed still. Something deeper in his head, touched.

The three lines weren't just randomly there. One pointed toward Iron Kiln Village. The one heading southeast was the farthest, reaching across the entire dry river valley, past the low rooftops of Gray Bridge, past roads he'd never walked. He felt the direction. No name for it. Later he'd learn: that direction was Calderis.

He stood by the wall for a long time. His mother called his name from beneath the bridge three times. The jar on the ground. Pickling liquid everywhere. A shame, that one. Radishes. Just right.

That night he lay in bed staring at the ceiling. Wood cracks, not blue lines. But blue light crawled through the cracks like mercury, flowing southeast. He pressed his hand to the brightest one. Closed his eyes. Gray Bridge's rooftops flashed past. The dry riverbed. Iron Kiln's ore piles. Three days of road. Then—

A city. Huge.

Built into the inner wall of a volcano. Lights packed into the bowl of the crater like someone had scooped up the night sky and dropped it into a pot. At the bottom of the bowl, a fissure, glowing pale blue. Same color as the water stains on his wall, but so much brighter. Like a blue sun that had burned out at the bottom of a bowl, leaving only embers behind.

He opened his eyes. Nothing on the ceiling. Wood cracks. Moonlight.

He lay there a moment. Then sat up, packed two changes of clothes, his mother's pickled vegetables, and a flask of water into a cloth bag. He still didn't know what that city was. But the blue light was calling his name, using the street name where his mother set up her stall. Gray Bridge's most ordinary street. He'd called it by name for fifteen years. First time the blue lines said it back.

Fine. He'd go.

The next morning he told his mother the trade caravan was leaving for Calderis the day after tomorrow. He'd go along, see the big city, sell some pickles, see if he could apprentice somewhere. His mother looked at him for a moment. Then looked down and kept cutting vegetables. The knife hit the board at the same steady rhythm. "Don't eat too many pickles on the road. Salty."

There. That was all she had. The accident seven years ago had burned the goodbyes out of both of them. What was left was sodium content.

He finished moving the jars. Brought a bucket to the old blacksmith at the bridge. Picked up a whetstone from under the bridge. His knife had been dull for a while. Might not even need it. The caravan went to Calderis every two weeks. Leaving tomorrow.

That evening he went to see the wall. The three blue lines were still there. Two had dimmed. The one pointing southeast was brighter than ever. He pressed his palm flat against it. Not a touch. A goodbye.

He didn't look back when he left. But the blue lines flickered behind him. Once. Like a blink. Like someone at the bridge seeing him off.

Two days later he woke up in a caravan cart. Gray Bridge was gone. Ahead, Iron Kiln's ore piles. Beyond that, nothing he'd seen except in ceiling cracks. In his bag: a stone he'd pried off the wall. He touched it. Warm. The sun didn't make that kind of heat. The southeast was pulling it. Calderis.

The accident seven years ago — three whole days he couldn't remember. The townsfolk found him under the bridge, soaked. The riverbed had been dry for two hundred years. That night, he was soaked. No wounds, just a thin scar above his left eyebrow. Ask him anything, he couldn't remember. Where he'd been those three days — gone. Why he was wet — gone. The doctor said trauma. It would pass.

It didn't. Seven years.

Now in the caravan cart, the southeastern horizon held a patch of sky the wrong color. Not blue. A color he didn't recognize.

The stone trembled once in his hand, like a nod. He didn't look back. The last time he'd looked back, right after the accident, there'd been someone standing on the bridge. A figure he didn't know. Never saw them again in town. Those three days — maybe he hadn't vanished. Maybe he'd gone somewhere. Forgotten.

But not everything forgotten is lost. The blue lines remembered for him.

The cart jolted over gravel. The southeastern sky — that color he didn't recognize — was spreading.
