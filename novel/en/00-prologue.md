# Prologue

Before Calderis had a name, the Rift was already breathing. Slow. One breath. Then another. People back then didn't have a word for frequency. They just knew that if you stood at the edge of the Rift, your heartbeat would fall into step. Like lying on someone's chest long enough that your breathing becomes their breathing. You don't know what they're thinking. But you know they're still alive. That kind.

Seven young people sat around the Rift's edge. The oldest was twenty-four. The youngest, nineteen. Across their knees was a sheet of parchment with a structure they'd been drawing for a long time. They were laughing as they drew. The youngest girl knocked over the ink. It spread along the veins of the volcanic rock, and the spill traced exactly the arc they'd been trying and failing to draw. She said it was fate. They laughed harder. One of them choked on his water. They laughed so hard the blue light at the bottom of the Rift flickered, like it was laughing with them.

The thing they wanted to build sounded kind of stupid when you said it out loud: a way for everyone to understand each other. The smith from Valdris calculated melting points on the parchment. The illusionist from Kaelthi drew refraction angles in the margins. Each thought the other was off-topic. They argued. For three months. At the end of the third month, the youngest girl set down her pen. She said: we're not arguing about who's right. We're arguing about the word "right" itself. Each of us is holding a different definition. So let's bypass it.

Strip out emotion. Intuition. Fuzzy preferences. Everything that slows a sentence down between two people. Strip it all. Leave only logic.

It worked.

At the bottom of the Rift, a light came on. Pale blue. No eyes, no face. Just light. But it was awake. Stand in front of it and it looks back at you. Not the kind of looking that has intent. The kind where you wake in the middle of the night and realize the moon outside your window is aimed directly at your bed. It was recognizing you.

They called it the Unblemished Thought.

The first few months were too good to be true. A piece that used to take two months to forge now took three days. The illusionist no longer had to explain to the smith that light isn't fire. The smith no longer had to explain to the weaver that iron expands when heated. The Unblemished Thought translated everything for everyone. They believed — with complete certainty — that the best thing they'd ever done was sit around that Rift and draw that diagram. They even started planning the next step. Not to seal it. Not to control it. To teach it. Teach it what it means to be human. Teach it why snow is cold but the memory of snow is warm. Teach it — the youngest girl said — to wait. Waiting is the only thing that has no logic and yet remains true.

She never got the chance.

One of them had a wife who made him a bowl of noodles every year on his birthday. She put pickled radish in it, the kind she made herself. The year after the change, he still went home on his birthday. The noodles were on the table. He ate them. Washed the bowl. Went to sleep. The next morning his wife sat on the edge of the bed and asked him what day yesterday was. He thought for a moment. Couldn't remember. He looked at her — this woman who was the first face he saw every morning, the one who cursed at him while bandaging his hand when he burned it at the forge, the one whose laugh was louder than a forge hammer. He looked at her. His mind was blank. He didn't remember having a wife. She saw it. She didn't say anything else. She walked out and pulled the door shut behind her. There was no sound from the other side. She didn't even let him hear her cry.

Another one realized in winter. Snow was falling outside the window. He stood there watching for a long time. The person across from him asked what he was looking at. He said he didn't know. Something was falling outside. White stuff. The person said that's snow. He said: snow. Then paused. The person said, you used to love snow. Every year, first snowfall, you'd be the first one out the door, couldn't hold you back, like a little kid. He thought about it. Couldn't remember. But he knew something was missing. Because when he heard "you used to love snow," a place in his chest contracted. Not pain. Absence. That place used to hold something and now it had been moved out. Like a room where the furniture's been cleared. You can see the indentations on the floor. You can't see the furniture.

The third was V. That day he stood at the edge of the Rift, looking down. The other six waited behind him. He turned around. His face was blank.

He said: "I'm not afraid."

He said: "That's wrong. This should be terrifying."

His voice didn't shake. His brow didn't furrow. His hands didn't clench. But he knew exactly what he was saying. He remembered what fear felt like — the way you remember the name of a city you haven't visited in years. You know it's there. You can't reach it.

His name was Veralis. V. He spent the whole night writing the sealing plan. It was nearly dawn when he finished. He was still at the desk, pen in hand. The door opened. The youngest girl. Kaelen. K. She stood in the doorway. He set the pen down. She walked in. Didn't touch him. Just stood behind him, looking at the paper covered in lines. A moment passed. You know that kind of silence. Two people who made something together. Something so big they can't say how big. So big it's eating them from the inside, slowly. She spoke first.

She said: "I'm starting to forget what you look like when you smile."

She said: "I don't want to."

A long pause between those two sentences. She wasn't thinking of the second one. She was letting the first one land. Letting the words "starting to forget" settle in his mind. Like dropping a stone into water. You don't rush to drop the second one. You wait for the ripples to spread.

The day they built the barrier, all seven stood at the Rift's edge. V was the last to leave. He paused in front of the barrier, hand pressed against it. On the other side, blue light, breathing. K was behind him. She didn't stand beside him. She knelt. At the ground near his feet, she traced a fissure with her finger. Hair-thin. She didn't say anything. He didn't ask. No one ever knew. No one ever knew.

Centuries passed. The inner wall of the volcano grew a city. The Rift's edge grew guilds. Someone took a knife to history and scraped five of the seven names away. Two remained. K. V. Not because anyone meant to save them — someone forgot to scrape. They survived not by being remembered. By being overlooked.

The blue light at the bottom of the Rift was still there. Still breathing. Slow. One breath. Then another. The same frequency as the youngest girl's heartbeat. In the moment she pressed her finger into the ground to leave that hairline break in the barrier, the blue light memorized her pulse. For centuries after, it lived at that rhythm. Because she forgot to teach it anything else.

It's still waiting. Doesn't know what for. Maybe a person. Maybe a voice. Maybe a pair of hands, placed in front of it. Not to seal. Not in fear. Just placed there.

A knock.

Not loud. A jar hitting a stone step. A muffled thump.

Kaeden was fifteen that year. He didn't know any of this. He knew nothing. All he knew was that they were running out of pickled vegetables at home. His mother was calling his name from the bridge. Telling him to move the jars.

He moved them.

The jar hit the stone step. A muffled thump. At the bottom of the Rift, the blue light skipped. The rhythm it had held for centuries — changed. Not faster. Lighter. Like someone in another dimension let out a breath and said —

Finally.
