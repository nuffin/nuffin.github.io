# Chapter Fifteen: The Barrier

Thalen went to the Rift floor before dawn. The last time had been daytime, and he hadn't gone in. This time he told no one — not even Corvan. Some decisions you make alone.

The Rift floor in the early morning held no light. The crater's curvature kept the sun from reaching the bowl's bottom before nine. In the dawn dark, the Rift was a pure black fissure, its edges traced by deep blue cold light. Standing at the rim, looking down, you weren't seeing a fissure. You were watching something breathe. Today's breathing was faster than before Bonding Day. Just slightly. Enough. The Seal was loosening. Not today. Not Bonding Day. Seven years ago. Gray Bridge. Kaeden's accident. The first loosening hadn't happened at the bottom of the Rift. It had happened inside a child.

He descended. The Chaloris Guild had carved a narrow path into the rock wall — single file, iron chain for a handrail. The chain was ice in the morning. The Rift's inner walls never warmed. Halfway down, the cognitive barrier appeared. A surge of "no through way" rising from inside his own mind. Your brain telling you there's no path. Your instincts telling you turn back. Your memory telling you you've never been here — even though you passed this spot five seconds ago. He'd faced it many times. Each time he acknowledged it and kept walking. This time he stopped.

There were cracks in the barrier. Cognitive cracks, not physical — like spiderwebbing across glass before it shatters. On the other side of the cracks: a presence. Not human. Not creature. It was watching the barrier too. It knew someone had stopped on this side.

Thalen pressed his finger to the thinnest crack. Touching. Perceiving. Eyes closed. He learned three things. First: the barrier was built by the seven creators, but the cracks came later. Left by one person. Second: the cracks were Kaelen's. Third: after leaving them, Kaelen had erased her own name from every record. Not protecting herself. Protecting the fact that the cracks existed. If the cracks were discovered, they became evidence for her execution.

He pulled his hand back. Didn't go further. A few steps were enough. He saw the barrier's full shape: a ring, not a wall. Inside the ring: the "other side" of the Unblemished Thought — its dimension of pure-state existence, not physical space. Outside: the human world. The ring itself was a prohibition woven from the seven creators' own cognitive abilities. But Kaelen's cracks had altered the door. It was no longer absolutely sealed. It could be heard.

He turned back. The blue light swirled in the fissure. Compared to when he'd entered — one breath faster now. The Seal hadn't loosened further. His ability to perceive it had sharpened. The barrier was letting him see. Letting him — who had chosen not to enter — see what it was, what it guarded, what it feared.

He didn't return to the Strategy Court. He went to the abandoned testing station at the Rift's edge. Half-destroyed in the last accident, never rebuilt by Chaloris. Rubble walls. A stone chair blown into a corner. Someone was sitting in it. No footsteps. But someone was there. Didn't look like he'd just arrived. Looked like he'd never left.

Middle-aged. Eyes holding decades of tiredness. Plain gray coat, clean but not new. Nothing in front of him — no book, no tools. Just sitting. Watching the Rift's blue light.

"You saw the barrier."

Thalen didn't answer. Assessing — a person you couldn't assess by threat. No aggression. No defensiveness. Just stating a fact, like remarking the sky was gray.

"You saw the cracks. The cracks in the barrier aren't what matter. The person who left them — what was her name."

He hadn't said "what did you feel." He'd said "what was her name." Thalen was confirming — the presence on the other side. Two people whose names had been scraped away. K. V.

"You've been standing outside too long," Thalen said.

Seraphis didn't answer. He watched the blue light at the Rift floor — not the light itself, its frequency. Counting breaths. The same breaths Thalen had counted at the bottom. Seraphis had been counting for thirty years.

Thalen stood. "What did you see on the other side of the barrier."

Seraphis turned. First time looking directly at him. Eyes that had seen another dimension — like looking in a mirror and realizing the person in it wasn't your reflection, but someone making a different expression.

"A world. No good or evil. Only degrees. No memory. Only traces. Not hell. But not home either." He paused. "Because someone at home is waiting for you. Even if you've forgotten which direction home is."

He stood. The motion was slow. Thirty years of never needing to move fast. "You'll come back." Not an invitation. A statement. Like saying the Rift's blue light would still be breathing tomorrow.

Thalen turned and walked away. Faster than he'd come. He'd realized something. Corvan was waiting in the office with four files on the desk. Seraphis stayed at the broken station, still watching the blue light, still facing a direction he knew he could never fully return from. And the cracks in the barrier — after Thalen left — the blue light paused on them a moment. Then resumed breathing. One beat faster than before Bonding Day. Faster than any breath Seraphis had counted in thirty years.

It was waiting too.
