# Chapter Four: The Thing That Won't Stay Thrown Away

Nova had no guild. No surname. No parents. She'd survived fifteen years on the streets of Calderis by three things: stealing, selling, and running fast.

She knew she'd slept under thirty-seven different bridges. Some too loud. Some too cold. One had a dead cat floating under it. Her favorite was the third bridge from the Weavers' Alley. Morning sunlight slanted down from the rim of the volcano and hit her right in the face. Free alarm clock.

Lately the black market had been getting a new kind of stock. Old stuff. Faded scrolls whose ink blurred when you touched them — and glowed blue. Outdated tokens that used to open certain guild doors, now just flickering once in any socket. Broken tool shards whose edges weren't shattered. They were dismantled. Someone had taken them apart piece by piece and sold them as scrap. Twenty-some pieces for the price of three meals.

Nova bought a whole bag.

That night she spread the pieces across her bed of shipping crates and arranged them by size. She picked one up and set it next to another. The second piece lit up blue. She tried more combinations. Not random — specific pairings made the blue light flare, and the lit pieces would move on their own. Like magnets. Like they recognized each other.

She tried every combination. Stepped back three paces. The pieces had arranged themselves into a pattern. An eye. Open. The pupil was a vertical slit, identical to the Rift. The light wasn't on the pieces. It was in the space between them. A blue that didn't belong to any single shard. A blue that only existed when they were together.

Nova stared at the pattern for a long time. Her first reaction wasn't curiosity. It was cold, spreading from her spine to her fingertips. She had a gift — her gut knew things. Before danger fully formed, her body sensed it. This was how she'd stayed alive. Right now her gut was telling her: this wasn't junk.

She gathered the pieces up, wrapped them in a rag, and walked to the nearest bridge opening. Under the bridge ran an underground river — black water that had been flowing for who knows how long. She dropped the bundle. Plunk. Sank.

Went back. Slept.

Next morning. The bundle was beside her pillow. Dry. She opened it. All the pieces inside, arranged in exactly the same pattern as last night. Still glowing.

She tried twice more. Once she threw it upstream — the bundle floated back against the current. Once she shoved it into a passing merchant cart — the cart circled the city gate, and the driver found her at the bridge and handed it back. "You left this." She hadn't left it. She'd buried it under two bags of ore in the bottom of the cart.

She decided to stop throwing it away.

She stuffed the bundle into her pocket. The pieces vibrated softly. Not fear. Waiting. Like something was checking whether she was ready to know what it was waiting for.

Outside, the main roads of Calderis were being strung with festival banners. Bonding Day was coming. Two passersby walked past the bridge. One said the Rift Explorers were doing a public demonstration on the Abyssal Platform — supposedly they'd discovered a new cognitive architecture. The other snorted and said they claimed a new discovery every year. The first didn't answer.

Nova touched her pocket. The pieces trembled. She stood up, headed toward the black market. Not for the festival. To buy more pieces. She still hadn't traced the source, but one name kept surfacing in the market chatter. Not a seller. A courier. No guild. No one had seen his face. Just a hood, and a voice so low it sounded like it came from the back of his throat. He gave things away more often than he sold them.

Nova ran into him that evening. In the back alley behind the Weavers' Hall — a place even stray cats avoided. He passed her without stopping. But he said something.

"Rift Explorers' outer warehouse. Row three. Number forty-seven."

Then he vanished into the crowd. Nova didn't chase him. Anyone who'd lived fifteen years on the street knew: the things you get for free cost the most. But this time she didn't have a choice. When he said those words, every piece in her pocket vibrated at once. Not fear. Confirmation.
