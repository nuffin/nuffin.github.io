# Chapter Two: Meteorite

Orion discovered the iron was alive on his fifteenth birthday.

Iron Kiln Village lay a day's journey south of Calderis. The entire village lived by supplying metal to the city. Orion's father was the best smith there. Not best as in most skilled — best as in, when nobody else could fix it, they brought it to his shop. His father would take the thing, say nothing, turn it over in his hands, set it down. Next morning it would be on the doorstep, repaired. How he fixed it didn't matter. What mattered was that it worked again.

Orion had been helping in the forge since he could lift a hammer. He loved fire. Loved the sound of iron turning on the anvil. Loved the silence before his father's hammer fell. Not hesitation. Listening. Listening to what the iron told him in the flames. His father said every piece of iron came from underground, had been underground for millions of years. It had seen more than you. Listen to it before you strike.

On his birthday his father did not say happy birthday. He set a piece of ore on the anvil. Darker than usual, with fine veins across the surface. "From the Rift," he said. "Try it."

Orion clamped the ore into the forge fire. Heated it red. Brought the hammer down.

The ore didn't flatten. It released a light into the flames. Pale blue. Erupted from inside the ore and cast an image above the fire. A city. Built into the inner wall of a volcano, lights packed into the bowl of the crater. At the bottom, a fissure glowing the same pale blue.

The image lasted a heartbeat, then vanished.

Orion stood frozen. Hammer hanging midair. The forge fire danced in front of him, ordinary red. The ore on the anvil, ordinary stone. His father walked over. Looked at him. At the ore. At the fire. Picked up his own hammer. Brought it down. Clean. Decisive. The ore flattened. Ordinary iron. No blue light. No city.

"You saw it." His father said it flat. Not a question.

Orion didn't sleep that night. He lay in the small room behind the forge, staring at wood cracks in the ceiling, replaying the image over and over. Not the city itself. The light beneath it. For one moment the blue light and the ore had answered each other — the ore remembered where it came from.

The next morning he went digging through the ore piles behind the hill. Most of Iron Kiln's ore came from mines north of Calderis, the opposite direction from the Rift. But occasionally a merchant brought specialty stock from the southeast — pricier, better quality. He found five Rift ores, buried under the pile, covered in burlap. His father had hidden them.

He carried them back to his room. Fed them into the fire one at a time. No blue light right away — these ores weren't as ripe as the one his father had given him. But he discovered something: the rhythm of the hammer changed the response. Fast, continuous strikes made the ore flash briefly. Slow strikes, with several seconds between each blow, and the ore would glow on its own during the pauses. Not blue. Fragments of images in different colors. Rock veins deep underground. Ancient symbols. A color he had no name for. The images could be pieced together. They all pointed the same direction. Southeast. Calderis.

He hid the brightest piece — the meteorite — inside his shirt pocket.

Two months later, at dinner, his father said: "We're going to Calderis for Bonding Day. Showing samples. You're coming."

The night before they left, his father packed tools in the forge. Without turning around, he said: "When we get to Calderis, don't just look at the iron." Orion wanted to ask what he meant. His father had already turned in for the night. The forge fire dimmed slowly. The meteorite in his pocket grew warm. Not from body heat. On its own.

The caravan cart waited at the village entrance. A cart from the direction of Gray Bridge, with a thin boy sitting in it. Scar above his left eyebrow. Cloth bag on his shoulder. Their eyes crossed for a moment as the caravans met. No words. But Orion noticed: the boy was looking southeast. Same direction as the meteorite in his pocket.

The cart pulled away. Iron Kiln shrank behind them. Orion put his hand on the meteorite. It pulsed. Once. Again.

Slower than a heartbeat. Faster than the tide.
