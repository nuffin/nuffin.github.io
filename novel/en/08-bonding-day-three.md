# Chapter Eight: Bonding Day (Part Three)

Aella stood at the edge of the Platform for two hours. Her notebook was full — sixteen pages. Every page the same spiral. Starting from the edge, circling inward. The center was the Rift.

She wasn't recording the ceremony. She was recording what was actually happening. The probing sphere had started spinning thirty minutes ago. The blue light inside had shifted from slow pulse to steady rotation. Not fast. Wrong. Three months of reading the forbidden book, and one passage described the Seal's normal state: "The blue of the Seal breathes like a sleeper. Slow. Deep. It does not respond to any stimulus." The light inside the sphere wasn't breathing. It was answering. The sphere wasn't displaying the Rift's signal — it was talking to the Rift. Someone had put something inside the sphere that shouldn't be there. Or put a receiver at the bottom of the Rift. Or both.

Two in the afternoon. Guild delegates began taking their seats. Eight seats around the round stone table, one per guild. The Forge and Lantern delegates sat together — not planned, their chairs just happened to be adjacent. The Prism Studio delegate wore the brightest outfit of the day, color-shifting thread at the cuffs — she'd seen this person buy a saddle at the exhibition that morning. The Weavers' delegate sat silent in a corner, fingers weaving something into the air. Fine silver threads formed between her fingertips, then dissolved.

Then Thalen took his seat.

She knew him. Official records. The forbidden book. Every major Guild Council resolution bore his signature. But the man in front of her didn't match the signature. On paper, a bold stroke. In person, someone who looked at everyone and no one. His gaze distributed equally, never resting. A way of looking she'd only seen in Scriba. Not observation. Comprehension.

Thalen sat. The whole Platform went quiet. Not a command — his presence carried a weight that needed no words.

Probaris stood. Chief experimentalist of the Chaloris Guild. Tall, thin, white coat, at least three colored markers in his pocket. His voice wasn't loud. Every syllable reached every corner. The Platform's acoustics — cantilevered over the Rift — gave sound nothing to reflect against. Everyone heard the unamplified voice.

"Allow me to present—"

Aella closed her notebook. No need to record anymore. The sphere's blue light was accelerating — not spinning, pulsing. Like a blue heart. Like something waking up.

She scanned the crowd. Front row: the Gray Bridge boy, still there. He wasn't looking at the sphere. He was looking at things in the air. Blue lines. She could tell from his focal point — not seeing light, seeing light's path. Exhibition grounds direction: the blacksmith's apprentice, also front row. Hand on his chest. Pressing down something in his pocket that was glowing.

Then the forbidden book on her lap began turning its own pages. Not the wind. The wind came from below the Platform, rising. The pages turned backward, against the current. Stopped at chapter one. Stopped at the sentence.

The sphere cracked. A single line, impossibly thin and precise, like a scalpel incision. Blue light bled from the fissure — not light. Something between light and vapor. Slow. Heavy. Sinking toward the Rift.

She stood. The book's weight vanished from her lap. It was floating — not the whole book, just the first page, the one that should have been where the torn page was. A single sheet, thin, yellowed, separating itself from the book, hovering. Words on the back. Not Scriba's handwriting. Ink still fresh.

"Do not seal. Do not run. Do not pretend you don't see."

The blue light reached the bottom of the Rift.

Then everything changed.
