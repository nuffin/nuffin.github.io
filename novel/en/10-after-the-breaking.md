# Chapter Ten: After the Breaking

Three days after Bonding Day. Thalen sat alone in his office all night. The city model on his stone table had several dark spots. The Mother Forge's flames had weakened. The Forge Hall had reported a smith showing unexplained emotional loss. Several Lantern Tower spheres were flickering — not from all-nighters, but unstable citation networks, towers unsure where to move. The Rift edge showed three new cracks. Three.

Corvan pushed the door open at four in the morning. No knock. Thalen never locked it. Fifteen years of unspoken understanding between them. Corvan was the only person who didn't need an appointment, the only one who could walk in at four a.m. without being asked "what is it." Corvan only knocked when it was worth knocking. Only pushed the door when it was worth pushing.

He laid four files on the stone table. Spread apart, each occupying a corner.

"Gray Bridge." First file. "Kaeden. Fifteen. Farthest settlement in the Rift accident's impact radius seven years ago was Gray Bridge. He lost three days of memory. Started showing abnormal Rift-radiation perception about three months ago." Thalen opened the file. No birth certificate. No guild registration. Just a name and a date. The name was written on the back of a torn-off caravan manifest. The date: Kaeden's arrival at Calderis's outer gate on Bonding Day.

"Iron Kiln." Second file. "Orion. Fifteen. Smith's son. Father's the best craftsman in the village. In the past month, his father purchased unusual quantities of Rift-side ore. Not for smelting. Collecting. Hidden in abandoned mine pits behind the hill, covered in burlap." Thalen flipped to the second page. Last entry: a meteorite. Note: "Pulse frequency: 48 per minute. Slower than resting human heart rate. Faster than the tide."

"Lantern Tower." Third file. "Aella. Fifteen. Lowest-ranked copyist apprentice. Strung hundreds of anomalous citations with red thread in her basement. The resulting spiral points precisely at the Rift. Two months ago Scriba gave her a forbidden book." Thalen didn't open this file. Scriba's name was enough.

"Calderis streets." Fourth file. "Nova. Age unknown. She doesn't know either. No guild. Picked up a batch of blue-glowing scrap from the black market. On Bonding Day, the pieces reassembled into—" Corvan didn't finish. Thalen was already looking at the sphere's residual data. The symbol on the light curtain matched Nova's fragment pattern.

"What do they have in common."

"They all saw the symbol. And—" Corvan switched the data layer. Four dense blue-line trajectories, from four different edges of Calderis, converging on the Abyssal Platform. "—each of them was already detecting fragmentation phenomena before Bonding Day. Shortest: a month or two. Longest: possibly years."

Thalen looked at the four faces. Silhouettes from guild records. Thin boy from Gray Bridge, scar above his eyebrow. Broad-shouldered apprentice from Iron Kiln, forge calluses on his hands. Basement girl from the Lantern Tower, glasses repaired with copper wire. Skinny street girl, pockets fuller than her pack. All looking the same direction. Didn't know it yet.

"Find a middleman. Fundoris. The one called Lynx. Tell him I'm buying four pieces of information. No negotiation."

Corvan paused at the door. "What's your play this time."

Thalen looked at the four files on the table. Three new cracks at the Rift's edge, glowing faint blue on the model. Not at the bottom of the volcano. On his desk. In front of him.

"Not my play. Theirs. I'm just waiting — for them to find each other."

"And then."

"Then protect them. Before the other guilds do."

Corvan left. Thalen sat in his office. The four files caught the blue glow from the model. Five a.m. The sky outside the dome graded from violet to gray. The Prism Studio's first beam hit the dome, passed through the transparent ceiling, landed on the stone table — directly on the four files.

Thalen stared at the circle of light. Not coincidence. The Prism Studio's angles were exact. But exact itself was a word that took time to earn.

Under the stone table, where no one could see, he traced a line with his finger. Blue light flowed through his fingertip, leaving a hair-thin mark. He'd seen this mark at the bottom of the Rift. On the other side of the cognitive barrier. He didn't know who would hear it. But he knew: when the right person came, this mark would light up.

Dawn broke.
