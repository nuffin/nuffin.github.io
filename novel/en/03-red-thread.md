# Chapter Three: Red Thread

The Lantern Tower was not a tower. It was a cluster of towers, pressed together like giants whispering. Each tower had a light sphere floating at its peak, brightness shifting with the intensity of the work inside. A paper reaching its critical argument — the sphere blazed like noon. An experiment failing — it dimmed to candlelight. The corridors moved when you weren't paying attention, reorganizing by the citation relationships of the books on their shelves. Two books that cited each other would always end up adjacent. Two books that contradicted each other, as far apart as possible.

Aella was the lowest-ranked copyist apprentice at the Lantern Tower. Her job was sorting scattered document fragments — millions of them, unarchived. Her task was just to sort by catalog. But the catalog itself kept changing. A history book today might belong in the astrology section tomorrow.

Everyone else found this maddening. Aella found it interesting.

Two months ago she'd been sorting a batch of fragments transferred from the Northwest Tower. That tower's sphere had been dark for thirty years; these fragments hadn't been touched in longer than she'd been alive. She noticed something in them: some of the citation relationships were wrong. Fragment A cited Document B, but Document B didn't exist in the catalog. Fragments C and D shared the same handwriting but different signatures — one attributed to a Lantern Tower director from two hundred years ago, the other to an unknown copyist from a hundred years ago. Fragment E bore a date from before the Rift was sealed. According to official records, the word "seal" hadn't existed yet.

She pulled these problem fragments aside. Strung red thread between them — each thread marking an anomalous citation. Two months later, the wall in front of her was covered in red threads. The pattern they formed was a spiral. The center of the spiral pointed directly at the Rift.

Today her supervisor came to check her progress. A woman who'd spent thirty years at the Lantern Tower, most of it complaining about how fast the corridors moved. She stood in the doorway of Aella's basement, looked at the wall of red threads, at the piles of documents on the floor, at the books stacked to the ceiling in the corner. Then she said: "Two months. And all you've done is string."

Not a question. A verdict.

After the supervisor left, Aella sat among the books for a long time. Not hurt. Thinking. If the citation anomalies, the handwriting conflicts, the dating errors — if these weren't copying mistakes, but a systematic cover-up. Then her supervisor's reaction wasn't dismissal. It was a wall.

She was about to turn off the light and leave. At the end of the corridor, a figure appeared. Not walking — his four floating books rounded the corner first, then the man himself. Scriba. The Lantern Tower's chief archivist, a man perpetually surrounded by open books. He said nothing. Walked to her desk. Set down a book. Not one of his usual four. Older. Bound in a material that no longer existed. He opened it. Inside the first chapter, a bookmark. Same paper as the cover. A line of fresh ink, small handwriting.

"Bonding Day. The Abyssal Platform. Watch the demonstration."

Aella looked up. Scriba was already far down the corridor. He walked without sound. Not because he was light — the corridor was silencing his steps for him. The Lantern Tower maze always helped those it trusted.

She looked down at the book. The first line of the first chapter wasn't a title. Wasn't an introduction. It was a single sentence, separated from everything else by a blank line. The ink was heavier than the rest, as if the writer had paused, then returned with a steadier hand.

"The Seal is not made of stone or magic. The Seal is a lie — a lie to make every guild believe they should never go to the bottom of the Rift."

She turned the page. Torn out. Clean cut. A knife. She flipped to the back. Between the last page and the cover, a loose sheet that didn't belong to the book. One line. Same handwriting as the bookmark.

"Aella. You're closer to the answer than you think."

She closed the book. Outside the window, the Lantern Tower corridors drifted slowly. Tonight's tower arrangement was three — the light sphere on the southwest tower flickering, a group of scholars pulling an all-nighter on some argument. The sphere blazed like a small sun. But in Aella's basement, the shadows of the red threads trembled on the wall. The center of the spiral was giving off blue — not because she'd used blue ink. Light from the bottom of the Rift. Passing through the gaps in the documents. Through questions she hadn't figured out yet. Through the space between two red threads where she hadn't strung one yet.

She pulled a new red thread from her drawer. Held it. Didn't string it.

Not yet. But soon.

Bonding Day. The Abyssal Platform.
